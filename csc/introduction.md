# Computer Science - IT WAS NEVER ABOUT THE COMPUTER

If you've ever used a pencil.  
Good. Almost everyone.  
Now let me ask you something strange.  
When you used that pencil - were you studying wood?  
Were you there to learn about graphite? 
Did you go home and tell your parents, "Mom, Dad, today I learned about cedar trees"?

No. You used the pencil to write a story. 
To solve a math problem. To draw something you saw in your head that didn't exist anywhere else in the world yet.
The pencil was just the tool. The thinking was the point.

---

Now. I want you to look at these computers in front of you.

These little rectangles with chips and pins and tiny blinking lights.

Cute, right?

Here's what I need you to understand — and this might be the most important thing you hear today:
We are not here to learn about computers.

A man named Edsger Dijkstra — one of the greatest minds in the history of this field — said it perfectly.  
He said `computer science` is no more about `computers` than `astronomy` is about `telescopes` or `microscope` about `Biology`.  

Let that land for a second.

Biology study life, Astronomers study stars. They study black holes and galaxies and the birth of the universe.  
The telescope is just how they look.  
Take away the telescope — the curiosity is still there. The questions are still there.

Same thing here.

---

### What we are actually studying is something much older than any computer.

We are studying how to think.

How to break a big messy problem into small clean steps. How to notice patterns. How to say: if this happens, do that. If that fails, try this instead. How to build something out of pure logic that actually works in the real world.

That skill? That way of thinking? It's thousands of years old. It existed before electricity. Before silicon. Before screens.

The ancient Egyptians used it to build pyramids. Musicians use it to compose. Doctors use it to diagnose. Coaches use it to design plays.

We just happen to practice it on these little green boards.

---

Now let me tell you what I've watched happen in this program.

I've watched a kid who said "I'm not a math person" spend forty-five minutes debugging a single line of code — not because anyone told him to — but because he had to know why it wasn't working.

That's not a computer skill. That's persistence. That's intellectual courage.

I've watched a student who barely spoke in class stand up and explain to her teammates exactly why their sensor was reading the wrong temperature. Clearly. Confidently. Correctly.

That's not coding. That's communication. That's leadership.

I've watched students argue — genuinely argue — about the most elegant way to solve a problem. Not the fastest. Not the easiest. The most elegant.

That is aesthetic thinking. That is craft.

None of that lives inside the computer. All of it lives inside the student.

---

So parents — I want to speak to you directly for a moment.

I know some of you are thinking: is this practical? Will this help my child get a job?

Yes. Absolutely yes. The world runs on people who can think systematically.

But I want you to aim higher than that.

Because what your child is building here is not a resume line. It's a mental framework they will carry into every room they ever walk into for the rest of their life.

The kid who learns to debug code learns to stay calm when things go wrong. The kid who learns to break down a problem learns to handle complexity without panicking. The kid who learns that failure is just information — that kid is unstoppable.

---

And to the students — I'll leave you with this.

The most powerful thing in this room is not the WiFi. It's not the sensor. It's not even the code.

It's the moment when something doesn't work — and instead of giving up, you lean in and ask why.

That moment. That why. That is the whole thing.

The computer is just the telescope.

You are the astronomer.

Welcome to DCC.
